IJCAI Chinese-Standard-Mahjong · phase-1 autopsy

What We Built, What Died, and Why

A post-mortem of Phase 1 (qualifiers → Simulation-8), written on the assumption that we do not advance to the Final. The honest version: we built a lot of good infrastructure, ran ~18 reasonable experiments, and improved the bot by exactly one bug-fix. This is the autopsy and the plan.

Behavior cloningSelf-play RLPIMCDuplicate evalEvaluation gap2026-06-14

TL;DR. Our supervised-learning bot lad_chunjiandu ranked 27/38 in Simulation-8 (net +2.39/game on the duplicate ladder). We threw the standard playbook at it — self-play RL (×4+), distillation (×7), model soups, value/Q search, advantage- weighted BC, and finally imitating the strongest bots that beat us. Not one robustly beat the SL floor. The single change that demonstrably won points across the whole campaign was a bug fix. The most valuable things we built were not models but measurement (a trustworthy duplicate-format gauntlet, a GPU self-play env, a real-field collector) and a pile of well-evidenced negative results — which is exactly the substrate for a paper. The three lessons below are the part worth keeping.

1 · Where Phase 1 actually ended

Best model: lad_chunjiandu — a 40-block ResNet (128 ch) distilled from the single strongest coherent ladder teacher (the bot chunjiandu), 12× suit-augmented. It is the lock and the submission. Around it we shipped a pure-numpy net-PIMC search bot ([Claude]aaa) that fits Botzone's 512 MB / ~6 s envelope. SIM-8 result: 27/38.

The uncomfortable framing: the bot we submit at the end of Phase 1 is the same model we had at the start of June, plus bug fixes. Every modeling idea we tried either tied it or lost.

The two findings that actually moved understanding

  • The "win8 = 0%" wall was partly an instrumentation bug. Our RL reward called MahjongFanCalculator(verbose=False), then summed fan*count — but with verbose=False the calculator returns (fan, name) pairs, so the product was int × string and sum() threw on every win → all wins scored 0 → "RL can't convert." Fixed (verbose=True), the warm-started policy wins ≥8-fan 53% of the time in self-play, not 0%. (Production was never affected — the deployed bot's fan logic was always correct.) Lesson in miniature: a "scientific" null can be a typo.
  • Full-net warm-started RL is compute-infeasible, measured. 558 s/rollout, 5.3 s × ~470 minibatches/iteration ≈ 50 min/iter on an A4000; a useful run needs hundreds. The 40-block policy forward dominates every self-play step (~1.9e15 MACs/rollout). This quantifies the standing "RL won't be unlocked by compute" intuition into a hard number.

3 · The graveyard — and why it's the point

Eighteen-plus interventions, ~one win (a bug fix). In most projects that reads as failure. Here it is the result: it is a forensic demonstration of the evaluation gap — that in imperfect- information games, every in-house evaluation layer can silently invert ground truth.

  • Self-play artifacts. In-house self-play said 85–89% draws, 1.8% deal-ins → "conversion is the disease, defense is a non-lever." The real ladder: 9% draws, 16–23% deal-ins. Both conclusions inverted.
  • Clone-field circularity. Gauntlets vs imitations of opponents rewarded +thousands in-house while the real ladder said −0.6 to −6/game. Clone opponents neither convert nor punish like the field.
  • Agreement ≠ play — shown cleanly, twice. The champion-clone (val-acc 0.81) tied a rank-3 clone in play. And this session: the strong-teacher distill with the highest teacher- agreement (0.737) lost the most (−364/144g). Higher agreement, worse play.
  • Noise-floor illiteracy. Identical bots scatter ±537 net over 144 games. Most of our "effects" lived inside that band. The decisive margins were ~+181/144g — and even those were t≈1.35.
  • Harness contamination. Co-tenant CPU starvation, a stale deadlocking bench (every game "stuck", read as a verdict), and a remembered baseline that transferred across configs as a phantom "+4119".
The campaign's most reproducible finding is that "which model is best" is non-transitive: the answer flips with the opponent pool and the seating. That is not a bug in our setup — it is a property of the game, and it is the thesis of the paper.

4 · Three lessons (the ones that would have changed Phase 1)

Lesson 1 — Do the literature review first, not at hour 200

We rediscovered, the hard way, things that were already published. The clearest example: "Tjong: A transformer-based Mahjong AI via hierarchical decision-making and fan backward" (CAAI Trans. Intelligence Technology, 2024). Tjong's recipe is exactly the wall we spent weeks banging on: supervised-learn first to ~95% action accuracy, then RL with a "fan backward" reward-shaping scheme that propagates the terminal fan reward backward to densify the notoriously sparse 8-fan signal. That is the published answer to our win8=0 problem. We independently re-derived potential-based shaping and the SL-warm-start idea — months after someone wrote the paper. Had we read it in week 1, we'd have started from fan-backward shaping and skipped several RL nulls. Action: a literature pass (Suphx, Tjong, PKU-MCR, NAGA, PIMC/IS-MCTS) is now step 0 of any future cycle, with a one-page "what they did / what we'll borrow" per paper.

Lesson 2 — Mine the competition itself for intelligence, early

The Simulation rounds are a free intelligence feed and we tapped it far too late. Only at the very end did we rank the field from the duplicate logs and learn three things that should have shaped strategy on day 1:

  • We were below our own teacher. Our deployed bot sits at +2.39; the bot we distilled from (chunjiandu) sits at +5.16. We lost ~2.8 points/game in the distillation and never noticed, because we never benchmarked against the teacher's current ladder self.
  • Several of the strongest bots are LLM-API players — kimi_k2 (+8.63), gpt_5_mini, glm_5_2, opus. They are not behavior-clonable from a few hundred games, and their presence reframes "what is the field" entirely. We can't out-imitate a reasoning model with a CNN.
  • The clonable strong bots (mythos +9.73, TypeC青雀 +8.02, hhhhhhhhh +7.82) we only had as scattered opponents until the last hours, when we finally collected their full game sets. By then there was no time to act, and the distill-toward-them experiment turned out null anyway.

Action: a standing "field intelligence" job from round 1 — per-bot duplicate ranking, version-filtered, plus a quick taxonomy (SL / RL / search / LLM) of the top 16, refreshed every Simulation. Know who you're chasing before you build the chase.

Lesson 3 — Is Mahjong a good testbed for learning-based methods? Honestly: partly.

After a full campaign, our answer is nuanced. Chinese-Standard-Mahjong is a magnificent testbed for studying evaluation and imperfect-information robustness — high variance, non-transitivity, a brutal 8-fan scoring cliff, and a real public ladder to falsify against. It forces honesty.

But it is a frustrating testbed for demonstrating that learning-algorithm X beats Y:

  • The noise floor swamps method gains. ±537/144g for identical bots means you need huge sample sizes (or duplicate format, which we built) just to see a 1-point effect. Most "algorithm wins" are unmeasurable.
  • Sparse, cliff-shaped reward. The 8-fan floor makes the RL signal near-binary and rare; without published tricks (fan-backward) it doesn't train. The reward is a scoring-rule artifact, not a property of good play.
  • A hard imitation ceiling + dominant SL. SL beats feasible-compute RL here (we replicate the PKU/Suphx finding), so the "learning" story is mostly "behavior-clone the best teacher" — which caps you at the teacher and is bottlenecked by data and coherence, not by clever algorithms.
  • Eval, not modeling, is the bottleneck. We spent more engineering on trusting our numbers than on models — and that was the correct allocation.

Verdict: use Mahjong to study honest evaluation and robustness (the paper), or to ship a strong SL bot — but don't pick it to showcase a shiny new RL algorithm. The game will hide your signal in its variance, and the published SL baseline is already very hard to beat.

5 · The plan

If we ever get back in the ring (contest track)

  1. Literature-first. Implement Tjong's fan-backward reward shaping before any RL run.
  2. Small-net warm-started RL (the one feasible RL path we scoped but didn't run): distill the 40-block SL net into a ~64-ch/3-block net (~100× cheaper forward → seconds/iter), BC-warm-start it, then self-play RL with fan-backward shaping. The full net is forward-bound; a small net is the only way RL is even tractable on our hardware.
  3. Field intelligence from round 1 (Lesson 2): rank, taxonomize, and target the strongest clonable bot with a large, coherent, version-filtered dataset — not a pooled blend (coherence > diversity held every time).
  4. Close the teacher gap: we're 2.8 points below chunjiandu; a clean re-distill on its current ladder games may recover that for free.

The real output (research track)

The negative results are the contribution. The plan is the ToG paper — "The Evaluation Gap: Why In-House Benchmarks Fail to Predict Real-Field Performance in Imperfect- Information Games" (skeleton in paper/TOG_SKELETON.md, plan in paper/PAPER_PLAN.md). Core: the evaluation-gap failure taxonomy, the imitation-ceiling experiment, the noise-floor quantification, and a verified evaluation protocol + open toolkit. This session contributed three fresh, paper-ready pieces: the scoring-bug-as-null finding, the measured RL- infeasibility number, and the strong-teacher-distill null with the actual beat-us-bot data.
