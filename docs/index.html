Chasing the Ceiling

An honest log of building a competitive Mahjong AI — including the parts where the improvements turned out to be mirages, and what we learned to do instead.

Behavior cloningSelf-play RLDuplicate-format evalNon-transitivity2026-06-08

TL;DR. Our strong supervised-learning bot sits at rank ~14/69. We threw the standard playbook at it — RL, distillation, model soups, more data, even imitating the actual 2025 champion. None of it robustly beats the SL floor. The single change that demonstrably won points was a bug fix. The most useful thing we built was not a better model but a better way to measure — and it revealed that "which model is best" is non-transitive: the answer flips depending on who you test against. This post is the honest version of that story, and the plan it points to.

The game, and the floor

Chinese Standard Mahjong is scored in duplicate format: the same walls are replayed across seat permutations, so luck largely cancels and ranking reflects strategy. A bot gets ~1s/turn (6s for Python), 256–512 MB RAM, and a brutal asymmetry — one illegal move or wrong win is −30, dwarfing a typical hand.

The field's consensus (and the PKU organizers' own thesis) is blunt: at feasible compute, strong supervised learning beats reinforcement learning for this game. 2022–2023 saw no pure-RL team reach the top 16. Our base — a 40-block BatchNorm ResNet trained on 5.87M expert decisions, then distilled toward a top ladder bot (distill100b) — is squarely on that winning axis.

The win that was real: a −30 bug

Auditing our Simulation-7 games (rank 14/69, mean +5.43/game), 5 of our 7 game-ending penalties were our own wrong-wins (WH, −30 each). Every one traced to the same root cause: when our CHI was preempted by another player's higher-priority PENG, the bot's history-replay applied our chi anyway, silently corrupting its hand state until it declared a phantom winning hand.

The fix: apply a recorded claim only when the judge's next request confirms it went through. Verified three ways — all 5 real wrong-win states now pass, an old-vs-new diff showed 0 / 4,202 decision changes on clean games, and a control run attributed the residual replay noise to a torch version difference, not the fix.

No model change touched this. Correctness was worth more than any architecture we tried. Keep that in mind for the plan.

The mirage: every "improvement" tied the floor

We ran the full playbook and measured each candidate head-to-head against the floor through the official judge:

The recipe law that emerged: the generalizable signal lives in the first ~600 steps; longer fine-tunes memorize and lose play strength. And a sharper warning — the candidate with the highest agreement to the teacher was often the weakest in play. Agreement is a proxy; play gates.

The real discovery: "best" is non-transitive

V1 looked like a breakthrough — until we measured it three ways:

Eval poolVerdict
V1 vs its near-twin parent (300 games)tie (+36)
vs a gauntlet of our own diverse CNNsV1 wins (+538 vs +219)
vs imitations of the real 2025 finalistsdistill100b wins (+5471 vs +4975)

The sign of "V1 vs distill100b" flips depending on the opponent pool. Against near-twins they tie (the classic monoculture trap). Against one diverse pool V1 wins; against the most on-distribution pool — bots cloned from the actual final field — the floor wins, on 5 of 6 opponents.

Neither model is robustly better. The "improvement" was an artifact of who we measured against. This is non-transitivity, and in a game with 80+ mutually-exclusive scoring patterns it is the rule, not the exception.

What the 2025 data actually bought us

We collected 39,145 games from the 2025 final (16 distinct finalists) and built the eval we'd been missing: BC imitations of the real field as a gauntlet. It told us our 2025-champion-distilled candidate was the worst of the three — distilling on a stronger, on-distribution teacher still did not beat the floor. A clean negative, but a durable asset: per-agent decision sets and a real on-distribution yardstick for everything we try next.

The plan to win

The honest read: in-house training levers have hit the SL ceiling at our compute. So the plan stops chasing a better single policy and attacks the three things that aren't ceiling-bound.

1 · Correctness & robustness — pure, un-ceilinged points

The WH fix proved a guaranteed-points lever exists. Systematically audit every penalty path over the 39k-game replay corpus: rob-kong (抢杠和), last-tile (海底/河底), 杠上开花, seven-pairs, fan-validation edge cases, cold-start TLE, and memory near the 512 MB cap. Build a replay regression suite. Every bug closed is points the ceiling can't take back.

2 · Exploit the non-transitivity — an inference-time mixture

We have several diverse models that beat each other in different matchups. Non-transitivity means a mixture is strictly more robust than any single model against a varied field: average their logits per decision, or select per-game from the pool. Their errors decorrelate. This turns today's most confusing finding into a concrete edge — and it needs no new training.

3 · Test-time compute — the one untried ceiling-breaker

We use a fraction of the ~6s/turn budget. A determinized shallow search — sample opponent hands consistent with the visible discards, roll the policy forward, pick the highest-EV discard — adds strength on top of the policy without needing to beat the training ceiling. This is closest to what heavy-compute champions actually do.

4 · A stronger on-distribution SL base — staying on the winning axis

The one training lever still aligned with the field's winners: retrain the base on official + 2025-final data combined, modern recipe (already reaches val-acc 0.875). The 2025 games are the actual final distribution. Judge it on the finalist gauntlet, not on agreement.

0 · Get ground truth

Every local eval is a proxy. The only truth is the ladder. Ship the bug-fixed bot now and run an A/B on real ranked games — even 20 games outranks any gauntlet.

The meta-lesson. The biggest gains this campaign were not a smarter model but a more honest measurement and a fixed bug. When the search space is non-transitive, the team that measures against the real distribution and doesn't fool itself with proxy metrics wins — not the team that runs the most training jobs.
